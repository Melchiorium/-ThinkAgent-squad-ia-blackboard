# Evaluation Report - CareSync Version 1

Sources used: `prd.md`, `architecture.md`, `architecture.pdf`, `gtm.md`.

## Final Decision: REVISE

## Global Note: 5/10

## Executive Verdict

CareSync addresses a real and emotionally strong problem: families coordinating elder care need fewer missed appointments, fewer medication mistakes, and better shared visibility.

The proposal is more concrete than a generic care app, but it is still not investable as a full MVP build. It expands into secure document storage, caregiver permissions, compliance, audit trails, and high-reliability notifications before proving that families will switch from existing tools.

The right next step is a controlled concierge pilot, not a full platform build.

## Business Viability

The target user is credible: adult children coordinating care for elderly parents, especially across cities.

The wedge around appointments and medication reminders is sensible. The problem is that the proposal adds secure medical document storage into the MVP. That dramatically raises the trust and compliance bar before the core coordination value is proven.

CareSync also still has to beat existing habits: calls, WhatsApp, shared calendars, notes, and ad hoc family coordination. The switching trigger is plausible but not proven.

## Profitability Potential

The subscription model is plausible but underdeveloped.

Missing proof:

- expected monthly price
- willingness to pay
- family subscription conversion rate
- support cost for onboarding families
- compliance/security cost
- retention targets
- whether agencies/caregivers are a real paid segment or future speculation

A family subscription can work only if the product becomes part of a recurring care workflow. Satisfaction with a prototype will not be enough.

## Execution Complexity

Execution complexity is high for an early MVP because the proposal includes sensitive medical documents, permissions, audit trails, encrypted storage, caregiver roles, and 99% notification reliability.

The architecture PDF reinforces this complexity by showing document storage, document sharing lifecycle, audit trails, encryption, and support dashboards. These are serious operational commitments.

The strongest MVP should likely exclude document storage until appointment and reminder coordination are proven.

## Go-To-Market Realism

The GTM is directionally good: concierge pilot, founder-led demos, 30 families targeted, and a clear first activation loop.

But acquisition remains vague. "Specific areas with elderly populations" is not a channel. The plan needs one concrete sourcing path: caregiver association, employer caregiver group, local senior center, clinic partner, or family caregiver community.

The target of 15 active families after one month is reasonable for pilot learning, but not enough for a business decision.

## Competitive Risk

Competitive risk is meaningful. Alternatives include WhatsApp, Google Calendar, Apple Calendar, shared notes, medication reminder apps, caregiver portals, and agency tools.

CareSync's defensibility would come from care-specific workflow, trust, usability, and privacy. That is not yet proven.

## Major Risks

- Families do not switch from existing tools.
- Document storage raises compliance scope too early.
- Privacy concerns block adoption.
- Permissions and caregiver roles become more complex than assumed.
- Notification reliability failures damage trust.
- Subscription willingness-to-pay is unproven.
- Concierge onboarding may not scale economically.

## Final Recommendation

Revise before full build.

Pilot conditions:

- remove or strongly limit document storage in the first pilot
- select one geography and one acquisition channel
- recruit 15-30 families
- test dashboard, appointment scheduling, medication reminder, role permissions
- collect minimal sensitive data
- measure weekly active families, invited member activation, reminder completion, appointment usage, retention, stress reduction, and willingness to pay

CareSync is credible, but the current MVP scope is too compliance-heavy before proving switching and payment behavior.
