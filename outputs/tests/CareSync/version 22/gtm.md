## Go-To-Market Notes
- **Main market bottleneck:** this product does not yet have a proven reason families will switch from texts, calls, and shared calendars. The real bottleneck is not feature completeness; it is whether one exhausted organizer will consistently use a new coordination space when the existing stack is “good enough.”
- **Side of the market to secure first:** secure the **family organizer side first**. Caregivers and siblings matter, but they are invited participants. If the organizer does not adopt and keep updating the workspace, the network will not form.
- **2 to 3 structural GTM decisions:**
  1. **Launch as a founder-led concierge pilot, not a self-serve launch.**
  2. **Choose one narrow use case: coordination of one older parent’s recurring appointments, tasks, and reminders across one organizer + one sibling + one paid caregiver.**
  3. **Use one acquisition motion only: direct outreach to adult children already coordinating elder care, then onboard manually.**
- **Initial target audience:** adult children ages roughly 35–60 who are the primary care coordinator for one parent, already juggling appointments, reminders, and sibling updates, and who feel repeated follow-up pain.
- **Positioning:** “A private coordination space for one family managing care for one aging parent—so everyone knows what needs to happen, who owns it, and what changed.” Do not position it as a full health record or caregiving platform yet.
- **First acquisition motion:** founder-led outbound to high-intent prospects through local caregiver networks, elder-care support groups, community organizations, and personal referrals, followed by manual onboarding into a concierge pilot.
- **Operating assumptions for the first acquisition motion:**
  - each prospect is already actively coordinating care and feels current-tool fatigue
  - the organizer has authority to invite the care circle
  - the organizer is willing to spend 15–30 minutes in a manual setup
  - at least one sibling and one caregiver will participate if the organizer asks
  - the team can observe usage within 2–4 weeks
- **Switching trigger:** a family switches when coordination breakdowns are frequent enough that the organizer is repeatedly re-explaining the plan, missing follow-ups, or losing confidence that tasks and medications are being seen.
- **First activation loop:** organizer creates one care space → adds the care recipient and care circle → enters the next 3–5 critical appointments/tasks/reminders → participants acknowledge or update statuses → organizer sees one shared source of truth and returns to add the next event. The loop only works if the organizer gets immediate visible relief from repeated follow-up.
- **What must exist before public launch:**
  - a manually supported pilot cohort of real families
  - one clear launch segment
  - a reliable consent/access model
  - visible acknowledgement for critical reminders
  - evidence that at least some families return without prompting
- **What must be productized now:** shared care space, invite/access roles, task/reminder/calendar coordination, structured updates, acknowledgement/read visibility, audit trail, and basic consent/authority capture.
- **What can stay manual during the pilot:** onboarding, setup of the care plan, invite coaching, support for permission questions, participant follow-up, and market recruitment.

## Review Summary
The main launch challenge is not building the coordination workflow; it is proving that one stressed family organizer will actually adopt a dedicated tool instead of continuing with texts and spreadsheets. The recommended direction is a founder-led concierge pilot aimed at one organizer managing one elder care circle, with the smallest possible product surface needed to create visible ownership, trust, and repeat use.

## Build Vs Pilot Operations

### Must Be Productized Now
- One care space per elder
- Invite-based roles for organizer, family member, caregiver
- Shared calendar, task list, and reminder schedule
- Structured updates tied to tasks/appointments
- Basic document upload and retrieval
- Default visibility rules for core objects
- Audit trail of changes
- Organizer consent/authority acknowledgement
- Critical reminder acknowledgement or read-status
- Basic access revocation and invite correction

### Can Stay Manual Or Operational During Pilot
- Founder-led onboarding
- Manual setup of the first care plan
- Manual recruitment of pilot families
- Manual follow-up with invited participants
- Manual guidance on permission and consent questions
- Manual review of region-specific privacy expectations

### Deferred Until After Proof
- Full messaging/chat replacement
- Integrations with providers, pharmacies, or EHRs
- Advanced permission granularity
- Elder self-serve workflows
- Analytics dashboards
- Automation beyond basic reminders
- Agency-specific workflows
- Broader home healthcare services

## Critical Assumptions
- One adult child organizer is enough to initiate and maintain the workspace.
- The organizer can recruit at least one sibling and one caregiver into the workflow.
- Families value structured ownership and acknowledgement enough to change habits.
- The trust model is sufficient for sharing sensitive coordination data.
- Reminder acknowledgement creates a visible benefit that generic tools do not provide.

## Requested Changes
- Define one launch segment explicitly: adult child primary organizers managing one parent with one sibling and one paid caregiver in a concierge pilot.
- Add a concrete acquisition motion: founder-led outreach via caregiver/community/referral channels, not broad self-serve marketing.
- Add a demand threshold for the pilot, such as repeat weekly use or active return by the organizer and at least one invited participant within 2–4 weeks.
- Clarify the activation event as “next 3–5 critical items entered and acknowledged,” not general workspace setup.
- Specify the smallest proof condition: a meaningful share of pilot families returning without founder prompting and preferring the workspace over texts for the next care cycle.

## Risks
- Families say they like it but keep using texts and calls.
- Caregivers do not reliably participate, leaving the workspace orphaned.
- Trust concerns block sharing of sensitive family information.
- The product feels like extra admin work for the organizer.
- Reminder acknowledgement is not strong enough to create a habit or switching trigger.

## Open Questions
- Which single launch geography has the simplest workable privacy and consent baseline?
- What exact organizer behavior counts as “active use” in the pilot?
- What minimum participation from caregivers is required for the product to feel valuable?
- Is visible acknowledgement more important than reminder delivery alone?
- Will families tolerate a manual setup step if it saves follow-up work later?

## Why This Could Fail Even With Good Execution
Even with solid execution, the launch can fail if the product solves a real coordination problem but not an urgent enough one to displace existing habits. If the organizer experiences the tool as “nice to have” rather than “the only place I can keep everyone aligned,” adoption will stall and the care circle will never form into a durable network.
