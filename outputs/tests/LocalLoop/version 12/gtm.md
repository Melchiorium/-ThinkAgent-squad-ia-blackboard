## Go-To-Market Notes
- **Main market bottleneck:** supply density in the launch neighborhood. If the feed does not feel immediately useful, consumer acquisition will not convert, regardless of product quality.
- **Side of the market to secure first:** merchants, specifically a small set of approved independent coffee and lunch spots in one dense neighborhood. Consumer demand is secondary until the feed is credible.
- **Structural GTM decisions:**
  1. **Lead with a merchant-seeded pilot, not a consumer launch.** Founder-led merchant onboarding creates the initial inventory needed for users to see value.
  2. **Use one neighborhood and two repeat-visit categories only.** Coffee and lunch are the narrowest categories with enough frequency to test redemptions and repeat behavior.
  3. **Make the launch concierge-backed, not self-serve.** Manual curation and founder support are acceptable until the market proof exists.
- **Initial target audience:** people who live or work in the launch neighborhood and buy coffee or lunch at least 3–5 times per week.
- **Positioning:** “The fastest way to discover and redeem offers from vetted independent coffee and lunch spots in your neighborhood.”
- **Primary acquisition motion:** founder-led merchant seeding, followed by merchant-distributed invites to nearby customers.
- **Operating assumptions for the first acquisition motion:**
  - merchants are willing to join if onboarding is simple and low-risk
  - at least a small portion of merchants will actively share the app with existing customers
  - nearby users trust merchant-recommended discovery more than generic app-store acquisition
  - one neighborhood can produce enough approved listings to make the feed feel useful
- **Switching trigger:** a user can find a relevant nearby independent option faster than checking Google Maps/Yelp, and redeem one simple offer without friction; merchants see at least one attributable customer visit or redemption.
- **First activation loop:** user opens app → sees vetted nearby merchant → saves or redeems offer → merchant verifies visit → user returns for another nearby offer or loyalty reward.
- **What must exist before public launch:** enough approved merchants to make the feed feel curated, one working redemption flow in real merchant settings, and a basic merchant approval process to prevent low-trust listings.
- **What must be productized now:**
  - curated neighborhood feed
  - merchant profile page
  - one redemption mechanism
  - approval state for listed merchants
  - basic loyalty ledger
  - save/navigate actions
- **What can stay manual during the pilot:**
  - merchant recruiting
  - merchant vetting
  - content/copy editing
  - feed curation
  - redemption issue resolution
  - user onboarding help
  - merchant follow-up and retention outreach
- **What should stay deferred:** reviews, event feed, broader personalization, multi-city rollout, sponsored placements, social features, multiple redemption methods, and automated merchant marketing tools.

## Review Summary
The core launch challenge is not building discovery software; it is proving there is enough merchant supply in one neighborhood to make the app useful enough for consumers to try and redeem. The right GTM direction is a founder-led, merchant-seeded concierge pilot in one dense neighborhood, with coffee and lunch as the only categories, and merchant-distributed invites as the first acquisition motion.

## Critical Assumptions
- a dense neighborhood can support enough approved independent coffee and lunch merchants
- merchants will participate in a curated pilot without a complex sales process
- merchant-distributed invites can acquire the first users at low cost
- users will try the app if the feed is visibly better curated than general search
- one redemption flow is simple enough for real merchant staff to use reliably

## Requested Changes
- Specify the primary acquisition motion as **founder-led merchant seeding plus merchant-distributed invites**.
- Define the smallest credible launch audience as **people who live or work in one dense neighborhood and buy coffee or lunch frequently**.
- Add a launch threshold for merchant supply, such as a minimum number of approved merchants needed before consumer rollout.
- Clarify the first activation event as **browse → save/redeem → verified visit → repeat return**.
- State that public launch should not happen until one redemption flow works reliably in live merchant settings.

## Risks
- merchant supply remains too thin to create a useful feed
- merchants do not actively distribute invites to nearby customers
- users default back to Google Maps, Yelp, or social discovery
- redemption is awkward enough that merchants stop participating
- repeat usage does not emerge after the first visit

## Open Questions
- How many approved merchants are needed before the feed feels credible to first users?
- Which merchant-distributed channel will be used first: QR at checkout, receipt inserts, or staff verbal invite?
- What minimum merchant participation level counts as active distribution?
- What constitutes a successful first redemption rate in the pilot?
- What repeat-use threshold is required before expanding beyond the initial neighborhood?

## Why This Could Fail Even With Good Execution
Even with good execution, the project can fail if merchant density never becomes strong enough to make the feed feel distinctly better than existing tools. In that case, users may browse once but will not switch habits, and the app will remain a thin directory rather than a repeat-use neighborhood utility.
