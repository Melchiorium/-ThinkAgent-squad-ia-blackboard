## Go-To-Market Notes
- **Main market bottleneck:** LocalLoop does not yet have a supply or demand problem first; it has a **trust-and-density bottleneck**. The app will fail early if the launch neighborhood does not feel “alive” with enough credible merchants, fresh offers, and repeatable reasons to open the app.
- **Side of the market to secure first:** **Supply first, but only as a concierge-controlled pilot.** Secure a dense set of independent merchants in one neighborhood before trying to recruit broad consumer traffic.
- **2 to 3 structural GTM decisions that shape the launch:**
  1. **Founder-led concierge pilot only** for the first release; no scalable self-serve launch yet.
  2. **One neighborhood, one category wedge, one audience**: nearby workers/residents seeking coffee or lunch “right now.”
  3. **One merchant promise only:** “We’ll bring you nearby visits with one simple offer and basic loyalty tracking.”
- **Initial target audience:** Urban professionals and neighborhood workers who are within a short walking radius of the launch area and make same-day coffee/lunch decisions.
- **Positioning:** “The easiest way to discover what local independent place is worth going to right now in your neighborhood.”
- **First acquisition motion:** **Founder-led direct outreach and in-person merchant onboarding** to build supply, paired with **invitation-only consumer access** to people who live or work in the same neighborhood.
- **Operating assumptions for the first acquisition motion:**
  - A small number of merchants will join if onboarding is handled for them.
  - Consumers will try the app if it immediately shows a few credible, nearby options.
  - The neighborhood must be dense enough that results feel useful on day one.
  - Manual curation is acceptable if it keeps quality high.
- **Switching trigger:** Move from concierge pilot to broader self-serve only after you can show:
  - a stable merchant set in one neighborhood,
  - repeat consumer opens over multiple weeks,
  - at least some redemptions or saves that recur,
  - and merchants asking to stay live after the pilot month.
- **First activation loop:**  
  1. User opens app from the launch neighborhood.  
  2. Sees 3–7 relevant local options with one clear offer or loyalty hook.  
  3. Saves or redeems one merchant.  
  4. Returns because the app is refreshed with nearby, still-valid options and a simple repeat-visit reward.  
  5. Merchant sees at least one attributable visit or saved intent and stays active.
- **What must exist before public launch:**
  - a single launch neighborhood chosen and frozen,
  - enough merchant supply to avoid an empty feed,
  - at least one reliable redemption path,
  - refreshed merchant hours/offers,
  - a manual process for fixing stale listings quickly,
  - and a defined pilot group of users to invite.
- **What must be productized now vs manual during the pilot:**
  - **Must be productized now:** neighborhood feed, merchant profiles, offer display, save/favorite, one redemption flow, simple loyalty tracking, freshness/status indicators.
  - **Can stay manual during pilot:** merchant recruitment, onboarding, profile setup, offer curation, redemption exception handling, customer support, analytics review, user invitation, and merchant data cleanup.
  - **Deferred:** reviews, event discovery, advanced personalization, multi-city expansion, self-serve merchant onboarding, payments, broader category expansion.

## Review Summary
The launch challenge is not building a fuller discovery platform; it is proving that one dense neighborhood can sustain enough merchant supply and repeat user intent to feel useful. The recommended direction is a founder-led concierge pilot in one neighborhood with a narrow coffee/lunch audience, one redemption loop, and manual merchant operations until the market shows repeat engagement.

## Build Vs Pilot Operations

### Must Be Productized Now
- neighborhood-based merchant feed
- merchant profile pages
- offer display
- basic proximity and category filters
- save/favorite action
- one auditable redemption flow
- simple loyalty tracking
- freshness/status markers for listings

### Can Stay Manual Or Operational During Pilot
- merchant recruitment
- merchant onboarding and profile creation
- offer approval and curation
- consumer invitations
- redemption exception handling
- support and dispute handling
- merchant updates and data correction
- pilot analytics review

### Deferred Until After Proof
- self-serve merchant onboarding
- reviews and ratings
- event feed
- advanced personalization
- multiple redemption methods
- multi-city rollout tooling
- payments and checkout
- broader category expansion
- automated growth marketing

## Critical Assumptions
- A single neighborhood can produce enough merchant density to feel credible.
- Users in that area will open the app repeatedly for coffee/lunch decisions.
- Merchants will accept a manual concierge setup if it drives nearby visits.
- One redemption mechanism is enough to validate trust and usage.
- Fresh local offers are a stronger hook than generic discovery tools.

## Requested Changes
- Define one exact launch neighborhood and the minimum merchant count needed for the feed to look credible. [launch_focus]
- Commit the first launch motion to a founder-led concierge pilot with no self-serve consumer acquisition at launch. [market_motion]
- Narrow the first audience to people who live or work within walking distance of the launch neighborhood. [audience_narrowing]
- Define the first activation loop around “open → see 3–7 relevant places → save/redeem → return for fresh local options.” [activation_loop]
- Specify the repeat-visit incentive as a simple loyalty reward tied to one merchant or one visit pattern, not a broad universal wallet. [loyalty_loop]

## Risks
- The merchant feed may look empty or stale if density is insufficient. [supply_validation]
- Consumers may not see enough advantage over Google Maps or Yelp. [user_differentiation]
- Offer quality may be too uneven, hurting trust quickly. [offer_quality]
- Manual operations may be sustainable only for a very small pilot. [operations]
- Loyalty may not be strong enough to create repeat usage. [retention]

## Open Questions
- Which exact neighborhood is dense enough to support the pilot?
- How many merchants are needed before the app feels credible?
- What is the single redemption method that is easiest to audit in person?
- What repeat action should count as activation: save, redeem, or return visit?
- What merchant evidence will be strong enough to justify staying live after the pilot?

## Why This Could Fail Even With Good Execution
Even with strong execution, LocalLoop can fail if the chosen neighborhood does not generate enough organic daily intent around coffee and lunch. In that case, the product may be operationally neat but still feel unnecessary next to existing maps, search, and deal tools, making it impossible to build repeat usage.
