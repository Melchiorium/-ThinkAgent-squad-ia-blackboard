## Go-To-Market Notes
- **Main market bottleneck:** supply trust, not candidate acquisition. The launch will fail if companies do not believe the platform can deliver bounded, useful work without turning into free labor or a messy freelance board.
- **Side of the market to secure first:** organizations with a real mission and low legal/trust risk, then candidates. You need a small, curated supply pool before any broader demand generation.
- **Structural GTM decisions:**
  1. **Founder-led concierge marketplace first** in Paris, not self-serve. Every mission is reviewed and matched manually.
  2. **Narrow the supply to one mission class**: short, bounded, portfolio-friendly tasks with explicit deliverables and low ambiguity.
  3. **Sell trust and completion, not scale**: the product is a verified proof-of-work pilot, not an open marketplace.
- **Initial target audience:**  
  - **Supply side:** 10–15 Paris-based small companies, startups, and associations already connected to the founders, especially those with a clear one-off knowledge task and a willingness to co-design the format.  
  - **Demand side:** 20–30 recent graduates and career switchers in Paris who are actively applying to junior roles and need one credible proof-of-work artifact.
- **Positioning:** “Verified starter missions in Paris that help candidates earn credible proof-of-work while giving small organizations useful output from bounded tasks.”
- **First acquisition motion:** founder-led outbound to trusted Paris organizations, using warm intros and direct outreach, with a manual intake call and mission review before any candidate sourcing.
- **Operating assumptions for the first acquisition motion:**
  - each organization can be persuaded to propose or accept one narrow mission
  - the mission can be completed in 2–8 weeks with clear deliverables
  - the organization values useful output enough to tolerate manual handling
  - candidates will join if the mission is positioned as portfolio-building proof, not freelance work
- **Switching trigger:** a candidate should switch when they lack experience but need a concrete proof item for junior applications; an organization should switch when they need a bounded task completed without hiring or agency overhead.
- **First activation loop:**  
  1. organization submits a bounded mission  
  2. mission is approved manually  
  3. matched candidate completes it  
  4. organization confirms usefulness  
  5. candidate receives proof-of-work  
  6. proof-of-work is used in applications and referrals, bringing in the next candidate cohort
- **What must exist before public launch:**  
  - a strict Paris-only mission template  
  - manual approval and rejection rules  
  - a vetted first set of 5+ missions from trusted organizations  
  - a simple candidate application and matching flow  
  - proof-of-work output format that can be shown in job applications  
  - a legal review of mission wording for France  
- **What must be productized now:** mission intake, manual approval state, candidate application, basic matching queue, deliverable upload, completion confirmation, and proof-of-work issuance.
- **What can stay manual during the pilot:** sourcing organizations, screening missions, assigning candidates, handling disputes, reviewing deliverables, and legal review of wording.

## Review Summary
The main launch challenge is not building a marketplace; it is proving that a small set of Paris organizations will trust the format enough to post real missions and that candidates will complete them for credible proof-of-work. The right GTM direction is a founder-led, concierge pilot with a tightly constrained mission type and a small, trusted supply-first launch.

## Critical Assumptions
- Paris has enough concentrated demand and supply for a small curated pilot.
- A narrow mission format can prevent the product from becoming a low-quality freelance marketplace.
- Organizations will accept manual review in exchange for trust and quality.
- Candidates will value proof-of-work enough to complete short missions.
- The proof-of-work artifact will be seen as credible by at least some hiring managers or recruiters.

## Requested Changes
- Add a defined **pilot audience**: “Paris-based small companies, startups, and associations already known to the founders” [market_motion]
- Add a **single mission category** for launch, rather than a broad taxonomy [scope]
- Add a **pilot threshold** for supply and completion before expanding outreach [demand_validation]
- Add a **proof-of-work acceptance criterion** that tests whether the artifact is useful in real applications [quality_assurance]
- Add a **mission trust gate** that explicitly blocks unpaid labor-like or vague missions [privacy_trust]

## Risks
- Organizations may treat the platform as a source of cheap or free labor.
- Candidate supply may exist, but not convert into completions.
- The proof-of-work artifact may not be credible enough to influence hiring.
- Manual review may become too slow or inconsistent.
- Legal ambiguity in France may reduce willingness to participate.

## Open Questions
- Which exact mission type is the safest and most credible first wedge in Paris?
- What proof-of-work format do hiring managers actually value?
- Which organization segment is most likely to accept a concierge pilot first?
- What minimum compensation or recognition is required to keep missions ethical and attractive?
- What legal wording is required in France to keep the model safe?

## Why This Could Fail Even With Good Execution
Even with strong execution, the launch can fail if the market does not see short missions as a credible substitute for experience: organizations may not want to pay attention to a new format, and candidates may not believe the output will materially improve hiring outcomes. In that case, the platform becomes a well-run but low-demand pilot.
