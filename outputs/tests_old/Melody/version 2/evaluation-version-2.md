# Evaluation Report - Melody Version 2

## Final Decision: REVISE

## Global Note: 4/10

## Executive Verdict

Melody Version 2 is directionally more concrete than Version 1 because it introduces a local pilot audience, founder-led outreach, music community events, and a clearer need for legal validation around music preference data.

However, the proposal is still not investable. The dating market is unforgiving: local density, trust and safety, retention, and acquisition efficiency matter more than concept novelty. Version 2 still does not prove that music compatibility is strong enough to drive switching from existing dating apps.

There are also internal inconsistencies. The PRD acceptance criteria call for 1000 users and 500 active conversations in the first month, while the GTM describes a pilot of 20-50 users and 100 active users before launch. That mismatch makes the plan operationally unclear.

## Business Viability

The core insight is plausible: music taste can be an identity signal and a conversation starter in dating.

But the proposal still overstates the problem. Traditional dating apps may be superficial, but they already support prompts, Spotify-style profile signals, interests, events, and community-specific targeting. Melody must prove that music compatibility is a primary matching criterion, not just a nice profile detail.

The target segment is still too broad: singles aged 18-35 passionate about music. The GTM narrows it somewhat to 200-500 enthusiasts in local metropolitan areas, but it still does not choose one city, scene, genre, or community. For a dating product, local density is not optional. Without a very concentrated launch, the experience fails.

## Profitability Potential

The freemium subscription model remains speculative.

Potential premium features include advanced matchmaking and concert perks, plus possible venue advertising. These may become revenue streams later, but the proposal does not define:

- subscription price
- expected paid conversion
- customer acquisition cost
- retention target
- local density threshold
- event or venue partnership economics
- cost of moderation and safety operations
- cost of music or concert data access

Concert venue advertising is also premature. Venues will care about audience size and measurable attendance impact. Melody does not yet have either.

This may become monetizable only if Melody can build a dense local community with high engagement and repeat usage. That is not proven.

## Execution Complexity

The MVP is technically feasible, but it is still over-scoped for the riskiest assumption.

The proposal includes onboarding, compatibility scoring, profiles, messaging with moderation, concert discovery, local venue partnerships, and legal compliance for music data. That is a lot before proving that users care enough about music-based matching.

Messaging brings trust and safety requirements immediately. Dating products need reporting, blocking, moderation, abuse handling, and privacy from day one. The architecture mentions moderation, but does not define adequate safety flows.

The architecture also describes 80% message delivery as a minimum requirement. That is not acceptable for a messaging feature. If messaging exists, delivery must be highly reliable. This indicates weak operational seriousness around a core workflow.

Concert discovery should be treated as an experiment or manual activation hook, not as a core MVP dependency.

## Go-To-Market Realism

The GTM improves by proposing founder-led outreach, local music communities, colleges, social groups, venue partnerships, and meet-ups. This is the right direction for a niche dating product because it needs concentrated community density.

But the GTM is still not concrete enough:

- no city selected
- no music scene selected
- no channel owner
- no concrete community list
- no gender/orientation balance plan
- no safety/trust plan for in-person events
- no minimum density threshold for viable matching

The operating assumptions are also inconsistent. A 20-50 user pilot is useful for qualitative learning, but not enough to test dating matching liquidity. A meet-up with 100-200 attendees may be useful, but it is an event strategy, not proof of app retention.

The PRD goal of 1000 users and 500 conversations in month one is not realistic without a much stronger acquisition plan and launch geography.

## Competitive Risk

Competitive risk is very high.

Melody competes with:

- Tinder
- Bumble
- Hinge
- Feeld and other niche dating apps
- Spotify-linked profile features
- Instagram, TikTok, and local music communities
- concerts and offline social events

Large dating apps can copy music prompts or integrations quickly. Melody's only potential moat is a dense local music community and a better event-to-match loop. Version 2 gestures toward this but does not prove it.

## Major Risks

- Music compatibility is not strong enough to drive app switching.
- Local user density is insufficient for meaningful dating matches.
- Acquisition costs become too high in a competitive dating category.
- Messaging safety and moderation are underdeveloped.
- Compatibility scoring feels shallow or inaccurate.
- Concert discovery distracts from core dating validation.
- Venue partnerships do not materialize or do not drive retention.
- PRD and GTM success thresholds are inconsistent.
- Monetization is speculative and unsupported by user willingness to pay.

## What Improved In Version 2

- GTM is more locally oriented.
- Founder-led outreach and meet-ups are better than broad social media acquisition.
- Legal access to music preference data is correctly identified as a blocker.
- Messaging moderation and admin controls are at least acknowledged.
- The proposal now frames venue partnerships as part of activation.

## What Still Needs Revision

- Choose one launch city.
- Choose one music scene, genre, or community.
- Define minimum local user density for a meaningful pilot.
- Resolve the mismatch between PRD acceptance criteria and GTM pilot assumptions.
- Validate music compatibility before building full messaging and event discovery.
- Define trust and safety flows for dating interactions.
- Clarify music data access: manual input, Spotify-style import, or third-party API.
- Define monetization thresholds before assuming freemium viability.

## Final Recommendation

Revise before building a full MVP.

Proceed only with a narrow local validation pilot:

- one city
- one music community or genre scene
- 100-200 concentrated early profiles
- manual or simple music-preference onboarding
- simple compatibility scoring tested against user feedback
- no dependency on automated concert discovery
- strict safety basics: reporting, blocking, moderation, and event safety guidelines
- measure match acceptance, first-message rate, repeat sessions, retention, event attendance intent, and willingness to pay
- use one community acquisition channel before expanding

Melody Version 2 is more concrete, but it is still a fragile dating-market thesis. Decision: REVISE.
