# Evaluation Report - SkillBridge Version 1

## Final Decision: REVISE

## Global Note: 5/10

## Executive Verdict

SkillBridge addresses a real problem: graduates need credible experience, and small businesses often have short-term project needs. The proposal is more disciplined than a generic marketplace pitch because it includes a wedge, alternatives, a business model hypothesis, manual vetting, and readiness status.

However, the proposal is not yet investable. The core model relies on unpaid or low-paid work, which creates legal, ethical, reputational, and trust risks. The business model is also internally weak: the platform proposes charging small businesses while simultaneously assuming they participate because the work is low-cost or unpaid.

The correct next step is a tightly controlled validation pilot, not a full MVP build.

## Business Viability

The user pain is real on the candidate side. Recent graduates and career switchers often need portfolio-quality work and references.

The harder question is whether small businesses will post enough high-quality, well-scoped projects. The proposal correctly identifies businesses as the side to secure first, but the business segment is still too broad. "Small businesses" can mean very different needs, budgets, sophistication, and willingness to supervise junior talent.

The candidate value proposition is plausible, but fragile. If projects are unpaid or low-paid, the experience must be clearly valuable, bounded, ethical, and legally compliant. Otherwise the platform risks becoming a channel for free labor.

## Profitability Potential

The proposed business model is a commission or fee charged to small businesses once a candidate is accepted. That is a starting hypothesis, but not yet credible.

Key gaps:

- no fee amount
- no expected conversion from project posting to accepted candidate
- no willingness-to-pay evidence from businesses
- no cost estimate for manually vetting projects
- no support/dispute cost estimate
- no legal/compliance cost estimate
- no repeat posting target

The central contradiction is that small businesses are presented as budget-constrained, yet expected to pay a fee for access to unpaid or low-paid talent. That may work in a narrow niche, but it must be proven.

Profitability is possible only if businesses repeatedly post projects and are willing to pay for quality, structure, and reduced hiring friction. The current proposal does not prove that.

## Execution Complexity

The technical scope is moderate but still too product-heavy for the riskiest assumptions. Profiles, project posting, matching, messaging, deliverable submission, reputation, admin review, dispute support, and compliance tracking are a lot to build before proving the market.

Several pieces can be manual in the first pilot:

- matching
- project vetting
- communication
- deliverable collection
- feedback gathering
- business onboarding

The architecture correctly includes admin approval, audit trails, and support mechanisms. That is good. But the legal and quality-control risk is operationally heavier than the technical build.

The proposal should not build a matching algorithm until manual matching proves repeatable.

## Go-To-Market Realism

The GTM has the right instinct: secure small businesses first, then bring candidates.

But the acquisition motion is still vague. "Manual outreach to local small businesses and business networks" needs a sharper wedge:

- which city or region
- which industry
- which project type
- how many businesses contacted
- expected response rate
- expected project approval rate
- who performs vetting
- what qualifies as a good project

The target of 10 businesses and 50 candidates is reasonable for a pilot, but the plan does not explain how those businesses will be convinced to post projects or pay fees.

University and career center channels may generate candidates, but candidates are not the scarce side. Business supply quality is the constraint.

## Competitive Risk

Competitive risk is high. SkillBridge overlaps with:

- internships
- freelance marketplaces
- volunteer platforms
- university project programs
- alumni networks
- job boards
- junior agencies
- LinkedIn and community matching

The product itself is not defensible. The possible differentiation is structured, vetted, short-term experience with clear deliverables and credible feedback. That differentiation depends on operational quality, not software.

The proposal does not yet prove a defensible acquisition or supply advantage.

## Major Risks

- Legal exposure from unpaid or low-paid work.
- Reputation damage if businesses use the platform for free labor.
- Low willingness to pay from budget-constrained small businesses.
- Candidate oversupply with too few quality projects.
- Poorly scoped projects leading to bad outcomes and weak reviews.
- High manual vetting and support burden.
- Matching activity without real employability outcomes.
- Weak differentiation from internships, freelance gigs, and university programs.

## What Is Strong

- Clear problem on the candidate side.
- Supply-first GTM direction.
- Manual vetting included.
- Web-first approach avoids mobile overbuild.
- Product, Tech, and GTM readiness are honestly marked `LIMITED`.
- The proposal identifies legal and quality risks instead of hiding them.

## What Must Improve

- Pick one launch segment and one project category.
- Define whether projects are unpaid, paid, stipend-based, or mixed.
- Define a legally safer project structure before launch.
- Prove business willingness to pay.
- Replace the matching algorithm with manual matching during the pilot.
- Define project quality criteria and rejection rules.
- Add employability outcome metrics, not just ratings and message response.
- Define repeat business metrics.

## Final Recommendation

Revise before any full MVP build.

Run a concierge pilot only if these conditions are met:

- one target geography
- one candidate segment
- one project category
- 10-15 manually recruited businesses
- projects manually vetted before candidates see them
- clear legal framing for compensation, scope, deliverables, and time commitment
- manual matching instead of algorithmic matching
- explicit test of business willingness to pay
- metrics for completion rate, candidate satisfaction, business satisfaction, repeat posting intent, and employability value

SkillBridge has a real thesis, but the current version is still a risky marketplace with unresolved legal and monetization problems. Decision: REVISE.
