# Evaluation Report - Melody Version 1

## Final Decision: REVISE

## Global Note: 4/10

## Executive Verdict

Melody has a clean concept: a dating app built around music compatibility, with concert discovery as an engagement hook. The proposal is focused, and the MVP scope is not wildly overbuilt.

However, this is not yet an investable opportunity. The dating market is brutally competitive, network-density dependent, and expensive to acquire into. The proposal does not prove that music compatibility is strong enough to pull users away from existing dating apps or sustain repeat usage.

The correct next step is concept validation and a small community pilot, not a full MVP build.

## Business Viability

The emotional insight is plausible. Music can be a strong identity signal, and some users may care deeply about shared music taste when dating.

But plausible is not enough. The proposal does not prove that music compatibility is a top dating selection criterion. It may be a conversation starter, not a reason to switch platforms.

The initial target user is also too broad: "singles aged 20-35 who identify deeply with music preferences" is not a sharp enough wedge. A better wedge would be tied to a specific local music scene, genre community, or city with enough live music density.

The product also faces a classic dating marketplace problem: without enough local users, matching quality collapses. Version 1 does not define the minimum local density needed to make the experience work.

## Profitability Potential

The freemium subscription hypothesis is weak at this stage.

Dating users are familiar with subscriptions, but they pay only when the app has enough attractive matches and clear advantage over alternatives. Advanced filtering, concert alerts, and exclusive community access are not yet proven premium features.

Missing profitability inputs:

- expected subscription price
- paid conversion target
- required local user density
- customer acquisition cost
- retention target
- match-to-message rate
- churn assumptions
- cost of concert data/API access
- moderation and trust/safety cost

Concert discovery might create affiliate or partnership opportunities later, but that is not developed in the proposal. Current profitability potential is speculative.

## Execution Complexity

The MVP is technically feasible: onboarding, preference capture, compatibility scoring, messaging, and concert discovery can be built.

But the scope includes two difficult products at once: dating and event discovery. Messaging also brings safety, moderation, abuse handling, reporting, and privacy responsibilities from day one.

The compatibility score is the core claim, yet the proposal does not define what makes it meaningfully better than simple shared artists or genres. If the score feels shallow, the product loses its differentiation immediately.

Concert discovery is useful, but it may distract from the core validation. It should be tested as an activation hook, not treated as a required platform pillar before proving matching value.

## Go-To-Market Realism

The GTM is too generic. "Social media and music communities" is a broad acquisition direction, not a concrete launch motion.

The dating category requires local density. Acquiring 300 sign-ups in a month is not meaningful unless they are concentrated by geography, age range, orientation/preferences, and music scene. A scattered user base will not produce matches.

The GTM should define:

- one city
- one or two music scenes or genres
- one acquisition channel
- one launch event or community wedge
- minimum active profiles needed before matching
- first-week match and messaging thresholds

The current plan risks getting sign-ups without usable dating liquidity.

## Competitive Risk

Competitive risk is extremely high.

Melody competes with:

- Tinder
- Bumble
- Hinge
- Feeld and niche dating apps
- Spotify-linked social features
- Instagram and TikTok discovery
- local music communities
- concerts and in-person events

Large dating apps can copy music prompts and Spotify-style integrations. Melody needs a community or local-density advantage, not just a feature.

The proposal does not yet define a defensible moat.

## Major Risks

- Music compatibility is not important enough to drive switching.
- Low local density prevents meaningful matches.
- Compatibility scoring feels shallow or inaccurate.
- Acquisition costs in dating are too high.
- Messaging creates trust and safety burden from day one.
- Concert discovery distracts from core dating validation.
- Premium subscription features are not compelling enough.
- Established dating apps can copy the core feature.

## What Is Strong

- Clear initial concept.
- MVP scope is relatively focused.
- Existing alternatives are identified.
- Readiness status is honestly marked `LIMITED`.
- The proposal recognizes the need to validate music compatibility before scaling.
- Concert discovery could be a useful activation or date-planning hook.

## What Must Improve

- Choose one launch geography.
- Choose one specific music community or scene.
- Define minimum local user density.
- Prove music compatibility is a meaningful dating criterion.
- Define the compatibility scoring logic.
- Test whether concert discovery improves match-to-message or date intent.
- Add trust and safety requirements for messaging.
- Define monetization thresholds before assuming freemium viability.

## Final Recommendation

Revise before building a full MVP.

Proceed only with a narrow validation pilot:

- one city
- one music scene or genre community
- landing page plus manual matching or lightweight prototype
- 100-200 concentrated profiles, not broad scattered sign-ups
- survey and behavioral test of music compatibility importance
- manual or simple compatibility scoring tested against user feedback
- measure profile completion, match acceptance, first-message rate, repeat sessions, and willingness to pay
- test whether concert recommendations increase conversation or date intent

Melody is an interesting niche, but the current proposal does not yet overcome the dating-market density and differentiation problem. Decision: REVISE.
